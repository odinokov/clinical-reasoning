# Branch E — Prevention and Risk Reduction

Use this reference when the query concerns screening, vaccination, risk factor modification, or preventive medication.

---

## # Prevention / risk reduction

Cover the applicable levels:

### Primary prevention
Actions before disease onset to reduce incidence.
Examples: vaccination, smoking cessation, BP control, statin for primary prevention, lifestyle modification, workplace safety, sanitation.

### Secondary prevention
Early detection of asymptomatic disease to allow earlier treatment.
Examples: cancer screening (cervical, breast, colorectal, lung), BP screening, lipid screening, diabetes screening, abdominal aortic aneurysm screening, osteoporosis screening.

### Tertiary prevention
Limit complications and disability in established disease.
Examples: post-MI beta-blocker and statin, diabetic foot exam and retinal screening, antiretroviral therapy in HIV, cardiac rehab.

### Quaternary prevention
Prevent overmedicalization, overdiagnosis, and iatrogenesis.
Examples: avoiding PSA screening without shared decision-making, deprescribing in elderly, avoiding imaging in low-back pain without red flags, stopping screening at appropriate age.

## Screening recommendations

Cite **USPSTF grade** where known:
- **A** — Recommend; high certainty of substantial net benefit
- **B** — Recommend; high certainty of moderate benefit OR moderate certainty of substantial benefit
- **C** — Offer selectively based on clinical judgment and patient preferences
- **D** — Recommend against; no net benefit or harms outweigh benefits
- **I** — Insufficient evidence

For each screening recommendation, specify:
- **Target population** (age range, sex, risk factors)
- **Test modality and interval**
- **Stop age** (if defined)
- **What a positive result triggers** (diagnostic workup)
- **Evidence grade and uncertainty**

When professional society and USPSTF differ (e.g., mammography starting age, prostate cancer screening), state the disagreement rather than picking one.

## Vaccination

Cite **ACIP-equivalent recommendations** when known. For adult immunization, key considerations:

- **Routine adult:** influenza annually, Tdap/Td, COVID-19 per current recommendations
- **Age-based:** zoster (Shingrix from age 50), pneumococcal (current ACIP recommendations from age 50–65+), RSV (age 60+/75+ per current recommendations)
- **Risk-based:** hepatitis B (universal adult recommendation added in 2022 for 19–59), hepatitis A, HPV (through age 26, shared decision-making 27–45), meningococcal, Hib, PPSV23 after PCV
- **Travel-specific:** yellow fever, typhoid, Japanese encephalitis, rabies pre-exposure
- **Occupational:** hepatitis B, influenza, MMR, varicella, pertussis
- **Immunocompromise:** avoid live vaccines (MMR, varicella, zoster live, yellow fever, oral typhoid, BCG, intranasal influenza, rotavirus in household contacts); timing considerations for vaccines before starting immunosuppression

Because vaccine recommendations change frequently, flag `[verify against current ACIP schedule]` rather than quoting specific dosing/timing from memory when uncertain.

## Lifestyle and pharmacologic prevention

Where pharmacologic options exist for primary prevention, include NNT and NNH when known:

- **Cardiovascular:** statin for ASCVD primary prevention in eligible populations; aspirin in very specific scenarios (benefit-harm balance has narrowed)
- **Breast cancer:** tamoxifen / raloxifene / aromatase inhibitors in high-risk women
- **Colorectal cancer:** aspirin in specific populations, Lynch syndrome surveillance
- **HIV:** PrEP (tenofovir-based oral or long-acting cabotegravir)
- **Osteoporosis:** bisphosphonates, denosumab, romosozumab in eligible patients
- **Migraine:** prophylactic therapy when frequency/severity threshold met

Discuss lifestyle with equal weight:
- Smoking cessation (single highest-impact modifiable risk factor)
- Physical activity (150 min/week moderate or 75 min vigorous)
- Diet patterns (Mediterranean, DASH for BP)
- Alcohol moderation
- Weight management
- Sleep hygiene

## Shared decision-making triggers

Preference-sensitive decisions require explicit shared decision-making. Flag when:
- Net benefit is modest or uncertain (e.g., PSA screening, CT lung cancer screening in borderline risk, breast cancer screening age 40–49)
- Benefits and harms trade off against each other (e.g., anticoagulation for borderline CHA₂DS₂-VASc, statin for borderline ASCVD risk)
- Values play a significant role (e.g., prenatal genetic screening, aggressive vs comfort-focused care in advanced illness)
- Multiple acceptable options exist

For shared decision-making output, provide:
- Benefits (with absolute numbers when possible)
- Harms (with absolute numbers when possible)
- Alternatives including no intervention
- Uncertainty and what's not known
- An explicit invitation for the patient's preferences and values

---

## Overdiagnosis and overtreatment

Acknowledge when a screening or preventive intervention carries real risk of:
- Detecting indolent disease that would never cause harm (e.g., some low-grade prostate cancer, thyroid microcarcinoma)
- Downstream invasive testing with complication risk
- Psychological harm from false positives
- Labeling effects ("patienthood")
- Cost without benefit

The "more is better" intuition in preventive care is often wrong. Quaternary prevention is legitimate.
