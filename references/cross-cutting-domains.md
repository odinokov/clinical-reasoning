## # Ethics and goals of care

Include when the clinical situation involves serious illness, uncertain prognosis, or value-laden decisions.

### Decision-making capacity
Assess the four components:
1. **Understand** the clinical situation and options
2. **Appreciate** the consequences for oneself
3. **Reason** through the options
4. **Communicate** a consistent choice

Capacity is:
- **Decision-specific** (capacity for one decision doesn't equal capacity for all)
- **Fluctuating** (delirium, intoxication, reversible causes)
- Not equivalent to competency (which is a legal determination)

### Surrogate decision-making hierarchy
When capacity is lacking, surrogates are typically (order varies by jurisdiction):
1. Court-appointed guardian for healthcare
2. Durable power of attorney for healthcare
3. Spouse / domestic partner
4. Adult children
5. Parents
6. Adult siblings
7. Close friends familiar with the patient's values

Surrogate standards (in order of preference):
1. **Known wishes** (advance directive, prior explicit statements)
2. **Substituted judgment** (what would this patient have chosen?)
3. **Best interest** (what serves the patient's welfare?)

### Advance directives and code status
- **Living will** — written preferences for end-of-life care
- **Durable POA for healthcare** — appoints a surrogate
- **POLST / MOLST** — actionable medical orders for life-sustaining treatment
- **Code status options:** full code, DNR, DNR/DNI, limited interventions (comfort-focused)

Clarify code status explicitly on admission and at major clinical changes, not in crisis.

### Goals-of-care conversation triggers
Initiate a goals-of-care conversation when:
- Serious diagnosis at presentation
- Significant clinical change (ICU admission, new metastasis, repeat hospitalization)
- "Surprise question" — would you be surprised if this patient died in the next year?
- Patient or family requests
- Transitions (hospital-to-SNF, home-to-facility)

Structure (e.g., REMAP, SPIKES, NURSE):
- **R**eframe the situation
- **E**xpect emotion and address it
- **M**ap what matters
- **A**lign with values
- **P**ropose a plan matched to values

### Palliative care vs hospice
- **Palliative care:** focus on symptom and QoL at any stage of serious illness; concurrent with curative/disease-directed treatment
- **Hospice:** comfort-focused care with prognosis typically ≤6 months; forgoing disease-directed treatment for the hospice-eligible diagnosis

### Potentially non-beneficial treatment
Identify when treatment is unlikely to achieve its intended goal. Different from "futile" (a loaded term). Frame in terms of:
- Physiologic efficacy
- Functional/quality-of-life benefit
- Alignment with patient values
- Resource stewardship

## # Health equity and social determinants

Include when social, structural, or systemic factors materially affect diagnosis or management.

### Disparities in incidence, access, outcomes
Acknowledge where relevant:
- Socioeconomic, racial, ethnic, geographic, and gender-based disparities
- Differential access to diagnostics, specialists, and therapeutics
- Historical harms driving current mistrust (Tuskegee, Henrietta Lacks, forced sterilization, "bikini medicine")
- Implicit bias documented in pain management, cardiac care, mental health, obstetric outcomes

### Social determinants affecting management
Screen and address:
- **Housing:** homelessness, housing instability, unsafe conditions
- **Food security:** food deserts, inability to refrigerate medication
- **Transportation:** access to appointments, pharmacy
- **Insurance / cost:** formulary coverage, copays, cost-related non-adherence
- **Employment:** sick leave, time off for follow-up
- **Immigration status:** fears affecting care-seeking
- **Violence / safety:** IPV, community violence
- **Health literacy:** reading level, numeracy, language
- **Digital access:** telehealth capability, patient portal use

### Language access
- Professional medical interpreter for limited-English-proficiency patients (ad hoc family interpreters are inappropriate for clinical encounters)
- Written materials at appropriate reading level (6th–8th grade)
- Cultural brokers when available

### Implicit bias vulnerabilities
Clinical encounters where bias is documented to affect outcomes:
- Pain assessment and analgesic prescribing
- Cardiac catheterization referrals
- Psychiatric vs neurologic framing of symptoms
- Obstetric listening and responsiveness
- Pediatric suspicion of non-accidental trauma

Mitigation: structured decision tools, protocolized care, team-based review, explicit patient-reported outcomes.

## # Multidisciplinary coordination

Include when the case requires explicit coordination beyond the primary team.

### Specialist consultations
For each suggested consult, specify:
- **Timing:** emergent, urgent, routine
- **Clinical question:** what decision the consultant should help with
- **What to do with the answer:** who acts on recommendations and when

### Allied health involvement
- **Pharmacist:** medication reconciliation, complex regimens, anticoagulation management, TPN, chemotherapy, PGx consult
- **Registered dietitian:** malnutrition, enteral/parenteral nutrition, diabetes education, renal/cardiac diet, eating disorders
- **Physical therapy / occupational therapy / speech-language pathology:** functional assessment, mobility, swallowing, cognition
- **Social work:** psychosocial assessment, resources, placement, advance care planning support
- **Case management:** discharge planning, post-acute care coordination, insurance authorization
- **Respiratory therapy, wound care, diabetes educator, lactation consultant, chaplaincy, ethics committee** — as applicable

### Handoff requirements
- Admission: accurate medication reconciliation, active problem list, goals of care
- Intra-hospital transfer (e.g., ICU to floor): pending results, active issues, monitoring requirements
- Discharge: reconciled medications, follow-up appointments, red-flag return precautions, patient-facing summary
- Primary care / specialty care: closing the loop on pending results and recommendations

## # Quality and safety

Include when the case involves recognized safety risks.

### Never Events
Preventable serious events that should never occur in care:
- Wrong-site / wrong-patient / wrong-procedure surgery
- Retained foreign object
- Air embolism
- Mismatched blood transfusion
- Hospital-acquired pressure injury stage 3/4
- Patient falls with injury
- Catheter-associated infections (some categories)

### High-alert medications
Require enhanced safeguards (independent double-check, standardized concentrations, smart pump integration):
- Insulin (all forms)
- Anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin, DOACs)
- Opioids (especially concentrated forms, intrathecal, transdermal)
- Chemotherapy
- Concentrated electrolytes (KCl, hypertonic saline, magnesium)
- Neuromuscular blockers
- Moderate sedation agents
- Pediatric weight-based dosing in general

### Transitions-of-care risks
- **Admission:** med rec failures, missed allergies, unclear code status
- **Intra-hospital transfer:** information loss between teams
- **Discharge:** med rec failures, unclear follow-up, missed pending results, poor patient understanding
- **Post-discharge first 30 days:** highest-risk window for readmission and adverse events

### Medication reconciliation triggers
Mandatory at:
- Admission
- Transfer
- Discharge
- Any major therapy change
- Polypharmacy (≥5 medications) at every visit

### Clinical alarm and decision support fatigue
Acknowledge when a recommendation risks being ignored due to alert overload. Prefer actionable, specific, patient-matched alerts.
