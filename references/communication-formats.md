# Clinical Communication Formats

Use this reference when the user requests written clinical communication.

---

## SOAP note

Standard format for progress notes and outpatient documentation.

```
S — Subjective
  History elements reported by the patient.
  Chief complaint, HPI, ROS, interval history.

O — Objective
  Vitals
  Physical exam findings
  Relevant labs, imaging, studies (new or interval)
  Medications and changes

A — Assessment
  Problem list with prioritization.
  For each problem: current status, differential if unresolved, clinical reasoning.

P — Plan
  For each problem: specific actions, medications, tests, consults, follow-up, disposition, patient education.
```

Guidance:
- Keep it organized by problem rather than chronologically
- Assessment should contain the thinking, not just a re-listing of the problem
- Plan should be specific enough for a covering clinician to act on

---

## SBAR handoff

For verbal or written handoffs, consults, and urgent communications.

```
S — Situation
  Who you are, who the patient is, what's happening right now in 1–2 sentences.
  "This is Dr. X from [service]. I'm calling about [patient] in [location]. They are [acute issue]."

B — Background
  Brief relevant history: admission reason, major comorbidities, relevant events this hospitalization.
  Keep to essentials. 3–5 sentences maximum.

A — Assessment
  Your clinical impression. What do you think is going on? What are you worried about?

R — Recommendation
  What you want from the listener. Be specific.
  Examples: "I'd like you to evaluate the patient in the next 30 minutes." "I need an order for X." "Can we transfer to a higher level of care?"
```

Guidance:
- SBAR is for efficiency — eliminate preamble
- State the ask explicitly; don't hint
- If urgent, say so up front

## Consult note

Structure for specialty consultation responses.

```
Reason for consultation
  The referring question, in one sentence.

History
  Focused HPI relevant to the consult question.
  Pertinent PMH, medications, allergies.
  ROS focused on the organ system or problem.

Examination
  Vitals and focused exam.

Data review
  Labs, imaging, pathology relevant to the question.
  Prior workup already completed.

Impression
  Clinical assessment with differential and reasoning.
  Address the referring question directly.

Recommendations
  Numbered list of specific actions.
  Include: additional workup, medication changes, specialist follow-up, monitoring parameters, contingencies.

Will follow / signing off
  Explicit statement of continued involvement or sign-off.
```

Guidance:
- The first sentence of the impression should address the referring question
- Recommendations should be actionable by the primary team without ambiguity
- State your follow-up plan clearly

## Admission H&P

Full history and physical for new admissions.

```
Chief complaint (patient's words in quotes)
HPI
PMH / PSH
Medications (reconciled, with doses)
Allergies (with reaction type)
Family history
Social history (substances, occupation, living situation, sexual, travel)
ROS
Physical exam (complete)
Labs / imaging / studies
Assessment and plan (by problem)
Disposition
Code status
```

---

## Discharge summary

```
Admission date / discharge date
Admitting diagnosis
Discharge diagnosis
Brief hospital course (narrative, 1–2 paragraphs)
Procedures performed
Consultations obtained
Discharge medications (reconciled, with changes from admission noted)
Pending results at discharge (with plan for who reviews)
Follow-up appointments with specific dates and providers
Activity restrictions and return precautions
Patient/family teaching provided
Condition at discharge
Disposition
```

Key safety elements:
- **Med reconciliation** must explicitly note what was added, continued, changed, stopped, and why
- **Pending results** must have a named accountable clinician
- **Return precautions** must be specific and patient-understandable

## Patient-friendly explanation

For direct patient communication (discharge instructions, test results, explanations).

Guidance:
- **Reading level:** 6th–8th grade (Flesch-Kincaid <8)
- **Avoid jargon:** "high blood pressure" not "hypertension"; "blood clot" not "thromboembolism"
- **Use analogies:** "Your kidneys are the body's filter..."
- **Active voice:** "Take this medicine every morning" not "This medication is to be taken..."
- **Chunk information:** one idea per sentence or short paragraph
- **Teach-back:** ask the patient to explain back in their own words
- **Actionable:** state what to do, what to watch for, when to return
- **Language-matched:** use professional interpreters; avoid family interpretation for clinical content

Structure for test result letters:
1. What we tested and why
2. What we found in plain language
3. What it means for you
4. What happens next
5. When to call us / when to return

## Shared decision-making conversation

When presenting preference-sensitive choices:

```
1. What is the decision we're making together?
   State the choice point plainly.

2. What are the options?
   Include: the proposed intervention, reasonable alternatives, and no intervention.

3. What are the benefits of each option?
   Use absolute numbers when possible. "Out of 100 people like you..."

4. What are the harms of each option?
   Same numerical framing. Include common mild harms and rare serious harms.

5. What is uncertain?
   Acknowledge gaps in evidence or applicability.

6. What matters to you?
   Invite values-based input explicitly.

7. What would you like to do?
   End with a concrete choice or plan to continue the conversation.
```

Guidance:
- Don't hide behind "what do you want to do?" before providing information
- Don't dictate the choice behind the veneer of "shared" decision-making
- If you have a recommendation, give it — but frame it as your recommendation, not the only option

## Goals-of-care conversation (REMAP)

```
R — Reframe
  "Given what's happened, I'd like to talk about what this means and what we should do now."

E — Expect emotion
  "This is a lot. How are you doing with all of this?"
  Use NURSE responses: Name, Understand, Respect, Support, Explore

M — Map what matters
  "As you think about the future, what's most important to you?"
  "If things got worse, what would be unacceptable?"

A — Align with values
  "It sounds like what matters most is [X, Y, Z]."

P — Propose a plan
  "Given what you've told me, I'd recommend we [plan]. What do you think?"
```

## Ethics consultation request

When requesting an ethics consult, include:
- Specific ethical question or dilemma
- Stakeholders and their positions
- What has been tried
- What decision or action is pending
- Timeframe for resolution
